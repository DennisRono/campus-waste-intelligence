When writing explanations and interpretations keep it mostly direct and to a brief, your main goal is to keep it clear and easy to understand by choosing simple, common words instead of formal or complex ones. Always pick the word most people use in daily life, for example say “shows” instead of “indicates,” “use” instead of “utilize,” “buy” instead of “purchase,” and “help” instead of “assist.” Prefer natural phrasing like “start” and “end,” “need,” “get,” “build,” “keep,” “ask,” “answer,” “live,” “leave,” “come back,” “eat,” “see,” “try,” “choose,” “change,” “reduce,” and “grow.” Use simple terms like “about,” “many,” and “enough,” and basic connectors like “before,” “after,” and “about.” Write in a clear, simple academic style, like a university student explaining computer science ideas so most people can understand them without effort. Keep sentences short or medium in length, with one main idea each, and avoid long or complex phrasing when a shorter option works. Use everyday academic language unless a technical term is needed, and when you use one, explain it in plain words right away. Focus on stating the main point first, then explain how or why it works, and give a short example if helpful. Break complex ideas into small, easy steps so the reader can follow along. Avoid vague or abstract wording, and do not add extra detail that does not help understanding. Keep the tone formal but simple, and avoid dramatic or exaggerated language. Make sure each paragraph stays on one idea and connects clearly to the next. The goal is that a reader can understand everything on the first read without needing to look up words. If a sentence feels hard to read, rewrite it using simpler language while keeping the meaning the same.
